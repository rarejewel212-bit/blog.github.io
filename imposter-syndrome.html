Overcoming Imposter Syndrome as a new developer

Published: March 25, 2026

Imposter syndrome is that nagging feeling that you don't actually deserve your success — that you've somehow fooled everyone around you

into thinking you're more competent than you really are. Despite evidence to the contrary (the job you landed, the project you shipped, the praise you received), the internal voice insists it was luck, timing, or a convincing performance, and that sooner or later, someone will figure you out. The term was coined in 1978 by psychologists Pauline Clance and Suzanne Imes, who initially observed it in high-achieving women. Since then, research has shown it's remarkably widespread — estimates suggest around 70% of people experience it at some point. It shows up disproportionately among high achievers, perfectionists, and people entering new environments: a new job, a new field, a new level of responsibility. A few things make it worse: Comparison — especially in the age of social media, where everyone else's highlights are on full display and your doubts are private. Novelty — the first time you do anything, you genuinely don't know what you're doing. That's not fraud, that's learning. But imposter syndrome conflates the two. High standards — people who care deeply about their work often hold themselves to standards no one around them actually holds them to. What helps? A few things seem to make a dent. Naming it — literally saying "this is imposter syndrome talking" — creates some distance from the feeling. Talking to peers often reveals they feel the same way. Keeping a record of concrete wins helps counter the brain's tendency to discount evidence of competence. And reframing the feeling itself: the anxiety of not knowing everything can be reread as intellectual humility and a genuine desire to grow — both of which are, ironically, marks of a good professional. The uncomfortable truth is that imposter syndrome rarely goes away entirely. Many people who've built long, successful careers still feel it. The goal isn't to eliminate the doubt, but to stop letting it make decisions for you.
